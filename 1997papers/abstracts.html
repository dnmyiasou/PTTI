Keynote Address

A New Seismographic System for Southern California;
The TriNet Project

Thomas H. Heaton
Professor of Engineering Seismology
Seismological Laboratory
California Institute of Technology
Pasadena, CA 91125

ABSTRACT

TriNet is a collaborative project of the California Institute of Technology, the California Division of Mines and Geology, and the U.S. Geological Survey to develop a digital seismographic network in southern California. TriNet will provide ground motion data having unprecedented frequency bandwidth and dynamic range from more than 600 stations located throughout southern California, including urbanized areas. All stations will have 3-component, high-dynamic-range, strong-motion force balance accelerometers. In addition, all stations will have either real-time or dial-up digital telemetry. Of the total 600 stations more than 200 of these will also have high-gain seismometers for measuring very small ground motions. Major products of the TriNet project are: 1) near-real-time monitoring and cataloging of earthquakes in southern California, 2) broad-band ground motions from distant earthquakes and other seismic sources, 3) strong-motion recordings from significant earthquakes in southern California, 4) near-real-time shaking intensity maps for emergency management, and 5) a pilot system for early warning of seismic shaking. Many other agencies (e.g., lifeline operators, emergency management agencies, etc.) are being included in the development of TriNet. TriNet will provide an unprecedented new capabilities to understand and to respond to earthquakes in southern California.

PROPOSALS FOR UPDATING TAI ALGORITHM

Claudine Thomas and Jacques Azoubib
Bureau International des Poids et Mesures
Pavillon de Breteuil
92312 Sèvres Cedex
France

ABSTRACT

The conditions of computation of TAI and UTC are in constant evolution. Since January 1996, access to TAI and UTC has been provided for the MJDs ending in 4 and 9, which corresponds to an update period of 5 days for these reference time scales instead of 10 days as was previously done. The replacement of clocks of older design by new ones of the HP5071A type, started in 1993, continues with consequent improvement in the stability of the free atomic time scale EAL, the first step in the calculation of TAI. The medium-term stability of EAL, expressed in terms of the Allan standard deviation y, is estimated to be 1.0×1015 for averaging times of between 1 and 2 months, a scale attribute of fundamental importance for institutions charged with the dissemination of real-time times scales.

To further improve the stability of EAL and to further reduce the delay of access to TAI and UTC, the algorithm which produces them may need to be revised. With this in view, experiments on real clock data collected at the BIPM have been carried out to show the advantage of simultaneously using an upper limit on relative weights rather then one on absolute weights, and a basic interval of computation of one month, rather than one of two months. Results of these tests are positive, so the BIPM reported on these studies to the Working group on TAI of the Comité Consultatif pour la Définition de la Seconde (CCDS) in view of implementing consequent changes in 1998. A decision is being made.

IS TAI ACCURACY DEGRADING?

Claudine Thomas
Bureau International des Poids et Mesures
Pavillon de Breteuil
92312 Sèvres Cedex
France

ABSTRACT

To characterize the accuracy of TAI, estimates are made of the relative departure (and of its uncertainty) of the duration of the TAI scale interval from the SI second as produced on the rotating geoid by primary frequency standards. The global treatment of measurements provided by individual standards led to a relative departure of 2.6×1014 with an uncertainty of 1.0×1014 for March-April 1997.

This relative departure is larger than that resulting from uniform application of the correction for the black-body radiation frequency shift (about 2×1014) in 1995, for which a procedure for compensation was applied immediately and still continues (cumulative frequency steering corrections, each of relative amplitude 1 to 2×1015 applied on dates separated by 60 day intervals). Current results suggest that this procedure did not even compensate for the apparent drift of the scale, observed by comparison with the continuously operating primary standard PTB CS2. This procedure should thus be reinforced, but without degrading the middle-term stability of the scale estimated to y ( = 40 d) = 1×1015.

The uncertainty of the relative departure of the duration of the TAI scale interval from the SI second on the rotating geoid is also larger than that expected from a global treatment of measurements provided by the best primary frequency standards, the standard uncertainties of which are sometimes in the low fifteens. This results from the estimates of TAI accuracy being based mainly on the measurements obtained from one single primary frequency standard, PTB CS2: unfortunately, since July 1996, the optically pumped frequency standard NIST-7 has provided only one measurement, no data has been available from the BNM-LPTF fountain FO1, and large frequency steps made data from PTB CS3 unusable. This situation is the worst we have experienced since 1994 and 1995 when five accurate primary frequency standards were in operation (PTB CS1, CS2, CS3, NIST-7 and LPTF-FO1), and before 1994 data were available from two standards (PTB CS1 and PTB CS2).

It follows that the BIPM does not receive a sufficient number of measurements from primary frequency standards for a safe estimation of the accuracy of TAI and of its evolution with time. The degradation of the accuracy of TAI may thus be only apparent. Results from new caesium fountains under development in a number of laboratories may help solve this problem.

RELATIONSHIPS BETWEEN DRIFT COEFFICIENTS
UNCERTAINTIES AND NOISE LEVELS:
APPLICATION TO TIME ERROR PREDICTION

Francois Vernotte, Michel Vincent
Observatoire de Besançon
41 bis avenue de l'Observatoire
BP 1615 - 25010 BESANCON Cedex
France

Oscillators are affected by drifts (linear phase drifts, linear frequency drift, i.e. quadratic phase drift) and different types of noise according to the power law model of power spectral density (from f-2 to f 2 frequency noise, i.e. f-4 to f0 phase noise). Generally, for long term instability characterization (duration greater than one hour), drifts coefficients are estimated by using least squares whereas noise levels are obtained from the residuals by using variances (AVAR, MVAR, TVAR, ...).

However, the low frequency noises, such as random walk FM, induce very long term fluctuations which may be confused with deterministic drifts. This effect, due to the non-stationarity of these noises, depends on the low cut-off frequency which must be introduced in order to insure power convergence for low frequencies. But, the real physical meaning of such a cut-off frequency is mysterious. In this paper, we propose another approach for the characterization of low frequency noises, considering a random walk FM as the integration of a white process since the setting up of the oscillator. We also calculate the standard deviation of "artificial" drifts due to long term random fluctuations, versus the noise levels.

The primary interest of these results concerns the estimation of the measurement uncertainty of drift coefficient: knowing the noise levels of an oscillator we calculate the standard deviation of the artificial drift coefficient due to these noises; thus, if a "real" deterministic drift is identified in the signal, its coefficients are determined plus or minus the artificial drift. The standard deviation of the artificial drift coefficients may be considered as the measurement uncertainty of the deterministic drift coefficient.

The secondary interest concerns the predictability of an oscillator affected by a deterministic drift. The reliability of a prediction depends on two factors:
-- the relevancy of the model,
-- the accuracy of the parameter determination.
Thus, the knowledge of the drift coefficient uncertainties yields a criterion for quantifying the reliability of a time error prediction.

Paper 5

TOTAL VARIANCE: A PROGRESS REPORT ON A NEW
FREQUENCY STABILITY CHARACTERIZATION

D.A. Howe
National Institute of Standards and Technology
325 Broadway
Boulder, CO 80303

C.A. Greenhall
Jet Propulsion Laboratory
California Institute of Technology

ABSTRACT

We present results of recent work on a newly developed frequency stability characterization, called Total variance, whose main advantages are improved confidence at and near the longest averaging time of tau=T/2, where T=data duration, and lower sensitivity to drift removal. By theory and simulation we compute its mean, variance, and probability distribution; in particular, we report its variance at tau=T/2 for the FM noises, and express the result as degrees of freedom. In the presence of white FM noise modulation, Total variance is an unbiased estimate of the Allan variance for all tau, and has three degrees of freedom at tau=T/2. We also find that simple bias formulas can be used in the presence of flicker FM and random walk FM.

We discuss Total variance's properties as a random variable that is functionally dependent on both tau and T, as contrasted with the theoretical Allan variance, which depends only on tau and whose conventional estimators are limited to tau<=T/2 for a phase record of duration T. In principle, Total variance can report values beyond the usual Allan variance last-tau value of tau=T/2; its values at tau>T/2 might be used to augment the normal last-tau value of frequency stability reported at tau=T/2.

We compute the frequency response of Total variance as a function of tau by averaging the squares of the Fourier transforms of Total variance sampling functions, and find that it resembles the frequency response of Allan variance. The results of these investigations indicate that Total variance, while it has an interpretation like that of the Allan variance, also has lower variability and less sensitivity to drift removal.

ABSTRACT

Much research has been conducted on GPS integrity during the last few years. Nevertheless, only a few studies deal with long-term experimental evaluation and none of them consider timing receivers. Moreover, typical integrity-dependent applications like civil aviation usually only require (at least) one "certified" positioning/timing datum within a certain time interval. This paper provides an overview of the whole setup and presents the most important results obtained from the analysis of the 778 MB of sampled data.

Poster 4

TOTAL VARIANCE AS AN EXACT ANALYSIS
OF THE SAMPLE VARIANCE

D.B. Percival
Applied Physics Laboratory
University of Washington
Seattle, WA 98106

D.A. Howe
National Institute of Standards and Technology

ABSTRACT

It is well known that the classical N-sample variance of mean frequency is dependent on the measurement duration, T, and does not converge for common kinds of random frequency instabilities as the data duration T gets longer. Instead, the recommendation is to report the square root of the two-sample (Allan) variance which is an RMS of first-differences of average frequencies taken sequentially. These coefficients are therefore the average frequency change vs. averaging time (tau), hence are a "tau domain" result but are commonly referred to as "time-domain frequency stability." A shortfall of these coefficients is that they are highly variable at large tau and hence difficult to interpret, especially if drift has been removed.

A new frequency-stability characterization called Total variance was introduced in 1994 which yields coefficients that can be directly interpreted like the Allan variance but with better confidence and less sensitivity to the method of drift removal. Total variance is in a different class of transforms to the tau domain in that it responds like a two-sample variance but its sampling of average frequency changes with tau in relation to T. Its square root (or Total deviation) is therefore not an RMS. Rather, Total variance is a random variable, that is, the one-shot result of an individual measurement dependent on tau and T instead of only tau as in the case of the theoretical Allan variance. Total variance can report values beyond the usual last-tau value of tau=T/2. In this paper, we discuss the relationship of Total variance to the classical N-sample variance. The coefficients beyond tau=T/2 are generally ignored, however we find that in fact all coefficients in Total variance exactly synthesizes twice the classical sample standard variance in contrast to the Allan variance which does not. This matrix result is not dependent on noise type. To this end, the assumptions using total variance are further validated.

We show how one can include all long-term Total variance coefficients into the last value usually reported, namely tau=T/2. By using the entirety of the Total variance, the familiar tau-domain frequency stability characterization becomes an exact analysis of the sample variance. This means that summing all the points in a Total variance plot will lead to exactly twice the sample variance much in the same way that integrating an estimate of a spectrum also yields the sample variance. We demonstrate these concepts by comparing Total variance to the Allan variance using data of NIST-7 versus a hydrogen maser.

ABSTRACT

A Motorola Oncore 8-channel GPS C/A-code receiver has been successfully tested for time meteorology applications, particularly in the one-channel mode. ABSTRACT

This paper describes the development and testing of enhanced time transfer processing software for the Motorola VP Oncore single frequency eight channel GPS receiver.

The processing software as developed generates a separate pseudorange residual for each satellite tracked, referred to a local time scale with outputs calculated each second. The software provides outputs in three different formats:

1. 13-minute averages for satellites matching the published BIPM tracking schedule;
2. five minute averages arranged in the ENSEMBLE history file format for all satellites tracked; and
3. raw 1 pps measurements for all satellites tracked.

The data can be transmitted automatically every day up to 20 recipients using standard e-mail.

The system as configured utilizes the Oncore receiver, an external time interval counter directly interfaced to the PC bus and a standard PC. The processing and multicast auto e-mailer software run in the Windows 95 operating system and would also run with minor changes in a number of other PC-based operating systems.

The receiver prototypes have been tested in short baseline common-view operation with the NIST standard receiver. The time difference between Oncore data (averaged according to the BIPM method to produce 13 minute tracks) and the NIST receiver is characterized by white phase noise with a magnitude of 2.7 ns RMS. Additional tests including sensitivity to temperature and common-view measurements using all satellites in view are planned.

The development effort is currently underway at NIST and is based on work started at Hewlett - Packard Laboratories. It is intended to provide a stable accurate low cost time transfer receiver for various applications which include the cooperative USNO-NIST Time Scale Test Bed (TSTB).

The TSTB will be initially established in the first quarter of 1998 with a network of six participating sites equipped with identical Oncore receivers exchanging common-view data daily via the Internet.

INTERNET TIMEKEEPING AROUND THE GLOBE

David L. Mills
University of Delaware
ABSTRACT


Timekeeping via the Internet has become a ubiquitous service extending to well over 100,000 public servers and clients in many countries of the world. In addition, an uncounted number of private servers and clients lurk behind the firewalls of many large government and corporate networks. There have been several previous studies which have compiled population and accuracy statistics using time-dissemination protocols such as the Network Time Protocol (NTP) and others. However, only one study considered reaching out to all hosts and routers in the global Internet. That study indexed the Domain Name System database of almost eight years ago. The newer studies used only a small population to evaluate architecture and algorithm improvements over the years since then.

Since the present day Internet has experienced an explosion of numbers, it is no longer acceptable or even possible to discover Internet hosts and routers by indexing a public directory service. However, it is possible to discover them for an organized subnetwork such as NTP. This paper presents a comprehensive survey of the NTP server and clients operating in the global Internet of today. While the search engine used in the survey could find only a fraction of all NTP outposts on the public Internet, it did find 182,000 client/server network paths used by 36,000 clients and servers; 1,700 of these acting as servers, and 230 of these synchronized to UTC via radio, satellite or modem. Uncounted others, including large numbers of personal workstations operating as broadcast clients, escaped detection. The survey measured the residual time and frequency errors of each server and client, as well as the time offsets of each client relative to its servers. The paper describes the sophisticated search engine, survey summary results, and conclusions drawn from the experiment.

SIPRNET NETWORK TIME SERVICE

Richard E. Schmidt
U.S. Naval Observatory
Time Service Department
Washington, DC 20392

ABSTRACT

SIPRNET, the Secret Internet Protocol Routing Network, is the Department of Defense (DoD) wide area network for encrypted hosts handling classified traffic. In 1997 the U.S. Naval Observatory (USNO) established network time synchronization service on SIPRNET, with dedicated servers providing Network Time Protocol (NTP) from the USNO Master Clock.

An overview of time server hardware and software will be presented, with details on system performance and accessibility.

Paper 31

THE APPLICATION OF NTP TO NAVY PLATFORMS

Karen F. O'Donoghue and David T. Marlow
Naval Surface Weapons Center
Dahlgren, VA 22448

ABSTRACT

Navy platforms consist of many systems ranging from navigation and ship control to tactical analysis, sensors, and weapons. These systems, each composed possibly of multiple computing elements are inherently time dependent. In addition, modern surface ships are quickly adding additional computing elements and communication networks to address emerging requirements and technology. Vital to making such a distributed system function in this environment is a stable robust time service.

A time service for today's surface combatants has several constraints. First, it must provide the level of synchronization required by the applications relying on it. Navy applications have quite stringent performance requirements. In addition, the time service needs to be cost effective, utilizing existing communication infrastructure and Commercial-Off-The-Shelf (COTS) components were possible. Finally, the time service must be scalable and fault tolerant.

The Navy has been investigating various methods for the provision of a time service on shipboard platforms. Early on, the Navy identified the Network Time Protocol (NTP) as a technology with potential to meet its needs. Various studies were conducted to analyze the performance of NTP on particular computing elements including modification of NTP parameters to better address Navy requirements. In addition, metrics were defined to measure aspects of the time service of interest to the Navy.

Navy efforts to date were applied to the High Performance Distributed Computing (HiPer-D) Program, a joint ARPA/Aegis effort. The HiPer-D testbed includes a heterogeneous computing base operating in a distributed fashion over multiple network technologies to provide actual Aegis Combat System functionality. To support this testbed, a time synchronization subnet was defined using both NTP and GPS components. Performance of the HiPer-D time synchronization subnet was analyzed based on the metrics previous defined.

This paper presents an overview of previous Navy efforts, a discussion of the metrics identified as applicable to the Navy environment, and a summary of the HiPer-D time synchronization subnet performance. In addition, this paper identifies ongoing and future work required to provide a stable robust time service and to fully support NTP in a shipboard environment.

Paper 32

BENEFITS AND ISSUES ON THE INTEGRATION
OF GPS WITH A WIRELESS COMMUNICATION LINK

Raymond DiEsposti and Steve Saks
The Aerospace Corporation
Los Angeles, California

ABSTRACT


Within the past few years, significant advances have been made in Global Positioning System (GPS) receiver technology and radio communication technology. Both GPS and cellular phone handheld devices have decreased in size, yet increased in capabilities and battery life. In both the civilian and military communities, GPS is being integrated with many other systems, for example, precise time keeping instruments, and tightly integrated GPS and Inertial Navigation Systems (INS). This paper analyzes the many benefits and discusses conceptual issues associated with a tight integration of GPS with a wireless communication link for both military and civilian users.

Paper 33

LONG-TERM TIME TRANSFER STABILITY
OF A FIBER OPTIC LINK

Otokar Buzek
Institute of Radio Engineering and Electronics of the Czech
Academy of Sciences Prague, Czech Republic

ABSTRACT

To ensure a subnanosecond timing over a distance of several tens of kilometers as is needed, for instance, in comparing the clocks of the Czech Time and Frequency Group Standard (CTFGS), an adequate transmission system should be used. Such a system must provide high group-delay stability, signal-to-noise ratio, and insensitiveness to the environmental electromagnetic perturbations. A time transfer link based on fiber optics seems to fulfill the above requirements.

Since 1994 fiber optic links have been used to ensure the traceability of four CTFGS Cesium Beam Clocks. The clocks are located at two separated laboratories which are mutually interconnected with four fiber optic links. Each link consists of an optical transmitter (lambda = 1300 nm), a single mode optic fiber of approximately 10 km, and an optical receiver. Five MHz signals generated by individual clocks are used to modulate the light to be transmitted from one laboratory to another. The main part (about 9 km) of the fiber is laid in a cable duct, the depth of which varies from 2 to 3 m, and a smaller part (800 m) is buried about 0.5 m underground.

Simultaneous hourly phase-time measurements have been carried out at both laboratories for more than 3 years. Environmental parameters such as temperature, air pressure, and humidity at critical points were registered. These measurements, besides their basic metrological purpose, have provided unique data to evaluate group delay changes in the links and to look for the sources of its instability in time intervals of one hour and longer.

The analysis of the data shows that the dominant variations have a seasonal character and are closely correlated with the cable duct temperature. These variations have been found to reach a peak-to-peak value of about 20 ns for a 20 km link with a maximum relative frequency deviation on the order of 1X10-15. The influence of other environmental factors on the transfer parameters of the fiber and on the link electronics are shown to be relatively smaller.

A method has been developed to correct mathematically the thermal group delay variations in the fiber. The method is based on the temperature response in the cable duct calculated from the outdoor temperature. After correcting for all temperature effects, 0.3 ns (one sigma) of residual variations have been reached.

Paper 34

TWO-WAY TIME TRANSFER THROUGH
2.4 GBIT/S OPTICAL SDH SYSTEM

Atsushi Imaoka and Masami Kihara
NTT Optical Network Systems Laboratories

Michito Imae and Kuniyasu Imamura
Communications Research Laboratory
Ministry of Posts and Telecommunications

ABSTRACT

An experiment to transfer time and frequency using 2.488 Gbit/s SDH Synchronous Digital Hierarchy (SDH) systems through 175 km optical fibers has been set up by CRL and NTT. The time of a Cs standard located in NTT is compared with UTC (CRL) by transmitting the time signals to each other and by measuring the time difference between the local clock and the other clocks. This system uses commercial optical fibers and seven repeaters in the 175 km transmission lines. The same devices to transfer time and measure the time difference are set at both CRL and NTT, and time difference data are also exchanged. The system uses the Section Overhead (SOH) to transfer timing and time signals, and time difference data.

The experiment started in November 1996 and the time difference has been continuously measured over 9 months. We confirmed that the frequency stability of the time comparison is 1x10-12 in the averaging time region tau greater 100s. This is equal to that of the Cs frequency standard (HP5071A) that is used in NTT. We previously measured the delay characteristics of another 2,400 km optical fiber system that isolated the frequency stability of the frequency standard, and confirmed that the time transfer measurement limitation in the optical fiber system is better than 1X10-14 in the averaging time region of 1X105s. These results show that 2.488 Gbit/s SDH time transfer has the time transfer capability that allows times to be compared to the level expected of the Cs frequency standard (HP5071A) in the averaging time region of 1000s. The time comparison results of this experiment agree well with the GPS common-view results. While these results do not show the time transfer capability in terms of long-term stability since they include the time variation between the NTT standard and UTC(CRL), the long-term stability in this system is expected to be better than 1 ns.

Paper 35

RESULTS FROM PROOF-OF-CONCEPT TIME
BASED COMMUNICATIONS TESTING

Tom Celano and S. R. Stein
Timing Solutions Corporation

Al Gifford
U.S. Naval Observatory

Eric Swanson, Roe Hemingway, and John Carney
Lincoln Laboratories

ABSTRACT

Time based communications is a concept where the synchronous layer of a communications channel is used as a vehicle for two-way time transfer. The technology originated in 1995 with prototype hardware designs for a self-calibrating optical two-way time transfer system. Point-to-point systems were built in 1996 and demonstrated synchronization error in a laboratory between a master clock and a recovered slave clock at the sub-nanosecond level over 10 km distribution distances. Since then, the communications payload capability has been added to the optical two-way system providing a true time based communications channel with two-way time transfer functionality embedded in a 155 Mbs data channel. In February 1997, two weeks of testing were performed at Lincoln Labs (MIT) to collect an extensive data set on the timing and communications performance of the system. The test objectives included:

a. Measure system performance with long range (>10 km) cable runs in a laboratory environment.

b.Establish timing and communications performance in a laboratory environment with typical communications hardware in the fiber link.

c. Characterize system performance using "real-world" (outdoor) links exhibiting temperature dynamics which change the length of the fiber.

d. Determine suitability of the system for application in future communications systems using All-Optical Network (AON) and wave-division multiplexing hardware.
This paper presents the results of the time based communications testing performed at Lincoln Labs (MIT) using an Optical Two-Way Time Transfer Communications System. A short explanation of system design and two-way time transfer implementation in a communications channel will be followed by a presentation of data sets from the Lincoln Labs testing. Time transfer measurement data will be presented from tests conducted in the laboratory as well as tests conducted over long distance, outdoor links. The laboratory measurements to be presented include two-way time transfer results over fiber in a controlled (constant temperature) environment with typical communications hardware inserted in the link between clocks. The hardware used for laboratory measurements includes electro-optic repeaters and erbium doped fiber optic amplifiers. The outdoor fiber links (intended to represent real world links) include a 75 km fiber run with OC-3 repeaters at the midpoint, and 100 km with wave-division multiplexing equipment in the link.

Synchronization error measurement data will be presented for each test configuration. Two-way delay data will be presented for the real world links to show the changes in fiber length induced by temperature variations in the outdoor environment. In addition, OC-3 bit error rate data will be presented to demonstrate the communications channel performance while conducting two-way time transfer.
