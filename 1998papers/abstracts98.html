GPS WEEK ROLL-OVER & Y2K COMPLIANCE FOR NBS-TYPE RECEIVERS

V. Zhang and M. Weiss
National Institute of Standards and Technology
Boulder, CO

E. Powers
U.S. Naval Observatory
Washington, D.C.

R. Loiler
Allen Osborne Associates
Westlake Village, CA

ABSTRACT

The NBS-type receiver software was modified to account for both the GPS week rollover and for the Y2K event. Receivers using this software have been tested by personnel from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) and the U.S. Naval Observatory (USNO) using a simulator at the Naval Research Laboratory (NRL). An independent test was performed by personnel at Allen Osborne Associates (AOA). The software now appears to be fully compliant with requirements for both the GPS week rollover and the Y2K events. Since the NBS-receivers are still the predominant receiver for time transfer among laboratories which generate International Atomic Time (TAI), this receiver was given significant attention. In the process, a number of lessons were learned about testing for these two events. These lessons are discussed in the paper. Testing techniques are presented based on these, including GPS simulator testing and worst-case event simulation.

Paper 4

1998: FIRST YEAR OF IMPLEMENTATION OF THE UPDATED TAI ALGORITHM

C. Thomas
Bureau International des Poids et Mesures
Sèvres, France

ABSTRACT

Following a proposal from the Bureau International des Poids et Mesures (BIPM), the Working Group on International Atomic Time (TAI) of the Comité Consultatif du Temps et des Fréquences (CCTF) decided in December 1997 that two changes should be brought to the algorithm used to compute TAI in order to improve the stability of the time scale and to reduce its delay of access. It followed that starting January 1 1998, TAI has been computed with implementation of:

  • an upper limit of relative weights, rather than one of absolute weights, and

  • a basic computation interval of one month, rather than two months.

In January 1998, the value of the upper limit of relative weight was set at 0.7% in continuity with the computation over November-December 1997, and it has not been changed since then. Definitive results of TAI computation are published each month.

The analysis of the results obtained since 1998 shows that, as expected, a stronger discrimination than before has been operated among the best clocks. Until the end of 1997, a HP5071A clock with a high performance tube was weighted at the upper weight as soon as it entered in TAI computation.

We observe that it is no longer the case, and that, in addition, it becomes more and more difficult for a clock to contribute at maximum weight because the total number of good clocks is always increasing, making the competition more severe.

The shortening of the interval of computation has already proven to be much more convenient for the users of TAI and for the BIPM, without any degradation of the metrological qualities of the time scale. This requires, however, a huge effort of the time laboratories which must send their data to the BIPM in due time, a recomputation after the deadline being impossible in this scheme.

Paper 6

STATISTICAL PROBLEMS IN THE ANALYSIS OF UNEQUALLY SPACED DATA

Patrizia Tavella
Istituto Elettrotecnico Nazionale
Torino, Italy

Mauro Leonardi
Politecnico di Torino
Torino, Italy

ABSTRACT

The statistical analysis of irregularly spaced data or the handling of series with missing data asks for particular care because results may be biased or strongly dependent on the chosen procedure. In particular, in PTTI applications, two points need to be addressed:

  • the noise analysis in terms of Allan or related variances,

  • the estimation of a missing datum on a particular date.

The study is performed by means of only analytical treatments by examining the case of TWSTT periodicity, where data are available on Mondays, Wednesdays, and Fridays. But the problems of handling unevenly spaced data are of more general interest, for example in the case of optical synchronization links, in the case of inter-satellite synchronization links, and in the case of the repeatability analysis of the new frequency standards which do not work continuously.

As far as noise analysis is concerned, the type and amount of noise are usually estimated by the use of the Allan or a related variance. Such estimation requires a series of equally spaced data. When data are not equally spaced they are usually filled in by means of some reconstruction technique such as a linear interpolation. The reconstruction technique induces a modification of the true noise of the data series, but it should be possible to identify the real noise, at least in some spectral bandwidth. By the introduction of the Allan covariance matrices and by using the transformation laws of such matrices, the effect of interpolating missing data is inferred with an analytical treatment and its effect on typical clock noise is illustrated.

The second task of interpolating a missing measure on a particular date is addressed with the double aim of smoothing the noise added by the comparison link and of retaining the clock noise for a correct evaluation of clock behavior. The evaluation of reconstructed data uncertainty has also to be approached. Different reconstruction techniques and, in particular, the use of a Kalman filter are evaluated.

Examples of application of the above procedures to some real measurements are reported.

Paper 7

THE RANGE COVERED BY A CLOCK TIME ERROR IN THE CASE OF WHITE FM NOISE

Patrizia Tavella
Istituto Elettrotecnico Nazionale G. Ferraris
Torino, Italy

Daniela Meo
Università di Torino
Torino, Italy

ABSTRACT

In past years, mostly due to the input of the telecommunication community, it became important to know the possible range spanned by the time error of a clock, since it helps in correctly designing the memory buffers. The problem may be illustrated as follows: suppose one has an atomic clock used as a synchronization unit in a telecommunication network and it is known that the clock signal is mostly affected by a certain random noise. Which is the "time error", i.e. the phase deviation, that such a clock may accumulate in a certain time interval? Apart from deterministic trends, the answer regarding the random component may only be a probabilistic one being that the nature of the process is stochastic. So the problem can be better expressed as: knowing the spectral density of phase fluctuations, which is the probability law of the range spanned by such phase fluctuations!

The case of white phase noise was recently examined and it was possible to infer the probability law of the spanned range. In this paper, the case of Gaussian white frequency modulation, which results in a phase random walk is considered and the range probability law is inferred, by the study of Wiener processes and their characteristics. This study yields an estimate of the "maximum" range that can be spanned by the clock error by the identification of a certain percentile in the range distribution, i.e., a range value which is not exceeded more than a certain small percentage of times.

A straight application to telecommunication follows because the inferred "maximum" range has a direct relationship with the quantity Maximum Time Interval Error (MTIE) largely used and debated in the telecom community and recently redefined as a percentile quantity. The study here presented supports the percentile definition and provides some materials for its understanding and its practical implementation.

Paper 9

FREQUENCY STABILITY OF ATOMIC CLOCKS USING KNOWLEDGE OF CROSS-CORRELATIONS

F. Torcaso, C.R. Ekstrom, E. Burt and D. Matsakis
U.S. Naval Observatory
Washington, D.C.

ABSTRACT

Presented in this paper are cesium and hydrogen maser frequency stability estimates for the clocks at the U.S. Naval Observatory. When an ensemble of clocks is uncorrelated, one can use the well-known N-cornered-hat method of Gray and Allan (1974) to obtain frequency stability estimates of individual clocks from time or frequency differences. If one incorrectly assumes the ensemble is uncorrelated, an N-cornered-hat method may yield negative frequency stabilities. Using recently developed analysis by Tavella and Premoli (1992) to allow for cross-correlation within an ensemble, we incorporate cross-correlations to obtain a mathematically consistent frequency stability for individual clocks.

Poster 1

AUTOMATIC FREQUENCY CALIBRATION USING A FUZZY LOGIC CONTROLLER

Ching-Haur Chang, Chia-Shu Liao, and Kun-Yuan Tu
National Standard Time & Frequency Laboratory
Telecommunication Laboratories of Chunghwa
Telecom Co., Ltd., Taiwan

ABSTRACT

This paper presents a way of frequency calibration using a Fuzzy Logic Controller (FLC). It is known that atomic clocks tend to drift due to aging or some environmental effects. This generally results in irregular changes of frequency offset. Due to this fact, it is not an easy job to determine a control quantity by which the frequency offset can be balanced. The new approach takes advantage of an FLC to determine the quantity. Two procedures are employed in our approach. First, the performance of the device being calibrated is collected at a predefined interval. Second, the FLC uses the collected data to compute a proper quantity and in turn applies it to the device to compensate for its drift. The FLC can compensate the device at any time whenever it is necessary since it is continuously being monitored. In the experiment, we use Frequency Measurement and Analysis System (FMAS) as the performance measurement equipment. Additionally, the FLC uses software written in C. Experimental results show that a cesium-beam oscillator with frequency accuracy equals to 1.4x10-13 can be improved to approximately 1x10-14 after a one week calibration.

ABSTRACT

We present the current status of millisecond pulsar timing observations at the Communications Research Laboratory (CRL). CRL has conducted the research on precise measurement of millisecond pulsar pulse timing. The main purpose is to improve the long term stability of present time scales by using millisecond pulsars. Here we introduce the outline of our observation system and observed data.

Generally, the signals from millisecond pulsars are very weak so that a large antenna and effective data processing are required for their precise timing observation. In addition, to the observations with the Kashima 34m antenna, we have had opportunities to use the Usuda 64m antenna which belongs to the Institute of Space and Astronautical Science (ISAS) with our observation system. Though available telescope time is quite limited, we have been able to detect PSR1855+09 at 2GHz. The data of PSR1937+21 and PSRT1713+07 with high Signal to Noise Ratio (SNR) are also effective in evaluating the performance of our system.

Besançon, France

ABSTRACT

The power spectral density of frequency fluctuations of an oscillator is generally modeled as a sum of power laws with integer exponents (from -2 to +2). However, a power law with a fractional exponent may exist. We propose a method for measuring such a noise level and determining the probability density of the exponent. UNEQUAL-ARMS MICHELSON INTERFEROMETERS

Massimo Tinto and J.W. Armstrong
Jet Propulsion Laboratory
Pasadena, CA

ABSTRACT

Michelson interferometers allow phase measurements many orders of magnitude below the phase stability of the laser light injected into their two almost equal-length arms. If, however, the two arms are unequal, the laser fluctuations cannot be removed by simply recombining the two beams. This is because the laser jitters experience different time delays in the two arms, and therefore cannot cancel out at the photo detector. The method presented in this paper requires a separate readout of the relative phase in each arm, made by interfering the returning beam in each arm with a fraction of the outgoing beam[1]. By linearly combining the two data sets with themselves, after they have been properly time shifted [2], we show that it is possible to construct a new data set that is free of laser fluctuations.

An application of this technique to future planned space-based laser interferometer detectors of gravitational radiation [3] is discussed.

PSEUDO RANDOM CODE CORRELATOR TIMING ERRORS DUE TO MULTIPATH IN CABLES AND OTHER CIRCUIT ELEMENTS

F. G. Ascarrunz
University of Colorado
Boulder, CO

T. E. Parker and S. R. Jefferts
National Institute of Standards and Technology
Boulder, CO

ABSTRACT

This paper analyzes how multi-path signal propagation inside components such as cables, filters and other circuit elements, can cause an amplification of the timing errors associated with the delay sensitivity to environmental effects in these devices. This multiplicative effect is due to the timing error in an early/late code correlator being a function of the relative RF carrier phase between the direct and multi-path signals. The theoretical analysis for the timing errors introduced by the correlator sensitivity to multi-path is well known [1,2], however the dependence of these correlator errors on the relative RF carrier phase has not been fully appreciated by the timing community.

Multi-path in a component such as a cable can occur when a mismatch in impedance occurs and a signal is reflected off the load back towards the source. If the backward propagating is reflected forward again it will become an interfering multi-path signal that is smaller in amplitude, delayed in time and with an arbitrary phase as compared with the direct signal. If the reflections are occurring at the ends of the cable then the multi-path signal is delayed from the direct signal by twice the propagation delay of the cable. Furthermore, the carrier phase difference between direct and multi-path signal is sensitive to small variations in this propagation delay.

Aging, temperature, and other environmental effects affect the propagation delay through the cable and give rise to larger than expected timing errors. For example consider a typical GPS timing receiver system that is composed of an antenna and LNA, a 30 meter low loss cable and a receiver located indoors. The carrier frequency is 1575 MHz and the C/A code has a chip rate of 1.023 MHz that corresponds to chip period of 978 ns. A good cable may have a delay temperature coefficient of 7ppm/°C. The propagation delay through

the 30 meter cable is 110 ns and will have variations on the order of 0.8ps/°C. Assuming that the multi-path signal in the cable is caused by impedance mismatch at the antenna

and receiver and that it is 30 dB attenuated with respect to the direct signal we can calculate the expected timing error for a 10 degree temperature rise of the cable. The propagation of the direct signal will change by 8ps and the propagation of the

multi-path signal will change by 16ps. The correlator, however, will indicate a change of -800 ps.

EFFECT OF CLOCK NOISE ON THE SYNCHRONIZATION PERFORMANCE OF CONSTANT BIT RATE (CBR) TRAFFIC IN AN ATM NETWORK

T. (Jay) Jayawarenda and Christopher Olszewski
AT&T Labs
Holmdel, NJ

ABSTRACT

An increasingly popular technique for transmission of voice, video and data traffic is the Asynchronous Transfer Mode (ATM), in which all transmitted information is divided into small segments and sent in 53-byte cells across an ATM network. The ability to carry different types of traffic simultaneously over a single common network is one of ATM’s main attractions.

When Constant Bit Rate (CBR) traffic with an associated frequency, i.e. bit rate, is carried over an ATM network, the frequency needs to be reconstructed at the exit node of the ATM network. One method for reconstructing this frequency is the Synchronous Residual Time Stamp (SRTS) method. This method relies on two synchronized clocks, that is, two clocks having the same average frequency, one at the entrance and the other at the exit of the ATM network. Bits of the incoming CBR traffic are periodically assigned a time stamp upon entrance to the ATM network. This time stamp is sent along with the information bits to the exit node of the ATM network. There, the time stamp and exit clock are used to recreate the frequency of the signal leaving the ATM network. Although cells are generated at the entrance node of the ATM network periodically, their transit times through the network are not constant due to Cell Delay Variation (CDV) encountered within the network. A key advantage of the SRTS scheme is that the synchronization performance of the signal exiting the ATM network is largely insensitive to CDV. However, the synchronization performance of the signal is affected by the behavior of the clocks at the entrance and exit of the ATM network.

This paper quantifies the synchronization performance of the recovered signal when different levels of noise are present in the signal source clock, the clock at the entrance to the ATM network, or the clock at the exit of the ATM network. Simulations were performed to study the effects of these noise sources separately and in combination. The SRTS scheme itself generates noise with a characteristic time deviation (TDEV) signature. As the noises of the clocks rise above certain levels, the inherent noise from the SRTS scheme becomes masked.
