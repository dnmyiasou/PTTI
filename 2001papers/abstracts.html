SPACE CLOCKS, WHY THEY ARE DIFFERENT

 

J. White and R. Beard

U.S. Naval Research Laboratory

 

ABSTRACT

 

Atomic clocks for use in operational satellites such as GPS and MILSTAR are a breed apart from their terrestrial cousins. Like most space electronic packages, clocks will seem to be a generation behind the technology used in other applications. The reasons for this include the need for high reliability parts, radiation hardness, and mechanical design. Other key drivers in the designs include zero gravity, unattended operation, limited monitoring bandwidth, and limits on weight, size, and power. Clocks used in short-term space experiments can be closer in design to ground clocks, but are generally not usable for operational systems without extensive modifications.

R. Lutwak, D. Emmons, M. Garvey, and P. Vlitas

Datum - Timing, Test & Measurement

 

ABSTRACT

 

We are developing an optically-pumped cesium beam frequency standard for use onboard the GPS-III satellite constellation.  The conventional cesium beam frequency standards, currently employed in GPS-IIF, are capable of a short-term stability of , limited by the shot noise of the atomic beam.  Significant performance improvement beyond this level would require an unacceptable reduction in the lifetime of the instrument. Paper 3

 

COMPACT, CONTINUOUS BEAM COLD ATOM CLOCK FOR SATELLITE APPLICATIONS

 

W. Buell and H. Wang

The Aerospace Corporation

 

ABSTRACT

 

Highly stable atomic frequency standards are of increasing importance for a variety of space applications, ranging from communication to navigation and time transfer to tests of fundamental science.  The requirements for an atomic clock vary significantly depending on the application, and for many space systems compactness and robust design are at a premium and stability dominates over absolute accuracy.  We report on progress with our design for a compact laser-cooled Cs beam atomic clock suitable for satellite applications. The basic design features a cold atomic beam source based on a single beam, conical-mirror, Magneto-Optic Trap (MOT) with a hole at the apex to produce a low velocity high flux atomic beam.  This cold atomic beam is then to be used in a laser-pumped Ramsey clock, with the clock signal derived from either a microwave C-field or, alternatively, by Raman resonance between the Ramsey fields. The cold atom source produces a continuous beam, which relaxes requirements on the local oscillator as compared with pulsed cold atom sources.  In order to reduce light shifts from the MOT light and improve signal-to-noise, the atomic beam is optically deflected and transversely cooled upon exiting the MOT's conical reflector. We estimate that the shot-noise-limited stability achievable with this physics package can be two to three orders of magnitude better than current cesium beam atomic clocks used in space applications.  We present our latest experimental progress towards a working frequency standard, including characterization of our six-beam magneto-optic cold atom trap and production & characterization of a cold atomic beam.

R. L. Tjoelker, E. Burt, S. Chung, R. Glaser, R. Hamell, L. Maleki, J. D. Prestage, N. Raouf, T. Radey, G. Sprague, B. Tucker, and B. Young

Jet Propulsion Laboratory

 

ABSTRACT

 

We will report on recent activities to develop a flight model of a small, low power, and low mass mercury trapped ion frequency standard leading to a flight demonstration on a GPS satellite. The small prototype standard design takes advantage of recent trapped ion frequency standard advances including using a nitrogen buffer gas for long vacuum pump life and a multi-pole ion trap to nearly eliminate sensitivity to the second order Doppler shift. AN EVALUATION OF VARIOUS SPACE CLOCKS FOR GPS IIF

 

V. Nuth, W. Feess, and A. Wu

The Aerospace Corporation

 

ABSTRACT

 

The SIS URE is a performance measure of the GPS.  It comprises clock random errors, clock errors due to the variations of temperature in orbit, ephemeris errors, and others. For this study, we use the SIS URE formula that requires the knowledge of clock errors and ephemeris errors in the radial, in-track, and cross-track components.  These errors are obtained by comparing the best estimates of satellite ephemeris and clock states of a Kalman filter against the NIMA precise ephemeris and clock estimates.  The filter uses data from both OCS and NIMA tracking stations, except that the estimated II/IIA/IIR clock data in the NIMA tracking data were replaced by the Aerospace’s simulated IIF Rb and Cs clock data.  The study examines various resulting SIS URE as a function of time (i.e., the root mean square or RMS of the entire GPS constellation) for the three simulated clock data for a two week GPS tracking period in October 1998.  The results show that all clock cases, except for the case of II/IIA Cs, can meet the threshold accuracy of 1.5 m for up to 24 hours AOD.  The study also shows a correlation of the SIS URE between the clock and the ephemeris.  Suggestions for improvement are also discussed.

Paper 7

 

SOLAR FLARES AND PRECISE SATELLITE TIMEKEEPING

 

J. Camparo and S. Moss

The Aerospace Corporation

 

ABSTRACT

 

On 14 July 2000 and 9 November 2000 two large solar flares occurred.  As measured by the GOES-8 and GOES-10 satellites, these flares were accompanied by an increase in the flux of energetic particles at geosynchronous altitudes.  Here, we discuss the effect of these flares on communication satellite timekeeping, specifically timekeeping onboard the MILSTAR FLT-1 and FLT-2 satellites.  FLT-1’s timekeeping device is a crystal oscillator clock, whose time-reading and oscillator frequency are tied through a satellite cross-link to the atomic clock carried onboard MILSTAR FLT-2.  It is well known that crystal oscillator clocks have sensitivity to radiation, while atomic clocks, like the one onboard FLT-2, are relatively insensitive to space radiation.  The solar flares had a noticeable impact on the FLT-1 quartz crystal oscillator, causing the oscillator frequency to change by ~ 2x10-10.  However, the flares had little if any observable effect on the FLT-2 Rb atomic clock.  Since the crystal oscillator's frequency was slaved via cross-links to FLT-2, the slaving procedure compensated for the radiation induced changes in the quartz crystal oscillator.  Consequently, cross-link slaving of FLT-1 to FLT-2 mitigated the influence of the flares on FLT-1’s actual timekeeping, and hence the satellite communications system.

LEAST SQUARES ANALYSIS OF TWO-WAY SATELLITE TIME AND FREQUENCY MEASUREMENTS

 

J. A. Davis, P.M. Harris, S. L. Shemar, and M. G. Cox

National Physical Laboratory, United Kingdom

 

ABSTRACT

 

Two-Way Satellite Time and Frequency Transfer (TWSTFT) has been used operationally by the international time and frequency community for several years. This paper describes new analysis techniques being developed for the processing of TWSTFT measurements.

The TWSTFT measurements are modeled in terms of time and normalized frequency offsets together with random errors considered to be a linear combination of the five well-known noise types encountered in time and frequency measurements. A method is described for analyzing the TWSTFT data to provide estimates of the time and normalized frequency offsets, together with their associated uncertainties, at any measurement epoch. The time series of TWSTFT measurements is first characterized through the use of second difference statistics. Reconstruction techniques are then used to estimate a “classical” covariance matrix for the time series of measurements from the second difference statistics. Finally, with the “classical” covariance matrix so obtained, Gauss-Markov estimation is used to provide unbiased and efficient estimates of the required offset parameters. The application of the technique to both the interpolation and extrapolation of TWSTFT measurements (and other similar) time series is discussed.

It is well known that TWSTFT data can be subject to occasional additional time offsets or “steps” resulting from replacement of the hardware underlying the collection of the data. In order to analyze the TWSTFT measurements as above, it is necessary to pre-process the data in order to remove such anomalous behavior when it occurs. A method is described for analyzing TWSTFT measurements to provide estimates of these steps, together with their associated uncertainties. The TWSTFT data in the neighborhood of the step is modeled using an empirical (polynomial) function augmented with parameters to represent the additional time offsets. Estimates of the polynomial coefficients and time-offset parameters are obtained from a least-squares analysis. Validation is undertaken to ensure a robust choice of polynomial degree.

P. Hetzel, T. Polewka,

Physikalisch-Technische Bundesanstalt, Braunschweig, Germany 

W. Klische, K+K Messtechnik GmbH, Germany

 

ABSTRACT

 

 The stability of two 2-channel fast-sequencing GPS receivers (Type K+K GPS6-2K made by K+K Messtechnik GmbH, Braunschweig, Germany) has been studied in co-location and in comparison with the NBS type receivers used for the regular Common-View (CV­) - time transfer. The new receivers consist of two channels (the "Tracking Channel" and the "Download Channel"), a built in time interval meter and a PC software. Once per second, all data obtained by the receiver are transmitted to the PC, which converts them into the desired format. Our currently used PC software generates the "GGTTS GPS/GLONASS Data Format" in either multichannel mode when observing all satellites in view, or in the traditional single channel mode when tracking a single satellite according to the BIPM schedule.

Two such receivers are under test now at the PTB. The differences UTC(PTB) - T(GPS) obtained with the K+K receivers are compared among themselves and with the corresponding results from the NBS type receivers. First results suggest that the K+K receivers have approximately the same stability as the NBS type receivers. Measured results will be presented at the Meeting.

ACCURACY AND PRECISION OF CARRIER PHASE CLOCK ESTIMATES

 

K. Senior and J. Ray

U.S. Naval Observatory

 

ABSTRACT

 

The accuracy of GPS-based clock estimates (modulo the instrumental calibration bias) is entirely determined by the pseudorange data.  When analyzing 24-hour arcs of global data sampled at 5-minute intervals, the formal error estimates for the clocks are normally about 115 ps, assuming each pseudorange observation has a measurement uncertainty of 1 m.  An internal test of the true measurement accuracy can be made by comparing clock estimates at the boundaries between analysis arcs for receivers equipped with very stable oscillators.  We have done such a test using the combined daily clock estimates provided by the IGS, after applying a time scale realignment based on a dynamically weighted ensemble of the IGS clocks to achieve higher time scale stability.

During the period 29 October 2000 to 2 June 2001, the observed day-boundary discontinuities have RMS values of 164 ps to 1281 ps for the 17 sites examined.  For some sites, infrequent but large outlier jumps are seen. The variation between sites is marked.  Since the same receiver and antenna models are common for many of these sites, it is likely that the dominant site-dependent effects are related more to the local pseudorange multipath environment rather than the hardware.  Notably, the two sites with the largest RMS discontinuities, ALGO and NRC2, display temporal variations that might have an annual period, although the available data span is currently insufficient to allow a reliable detection.  The largest jumps for ALGO and NRC2 are found during December 2000 to March 2001, which could be due to signal reflection off snow-covered surfaces near the antennas.

The precision of clock estimates within a given analysis arc is expected to be better than indicated by the formal errors or the accuracy measures because the relative clock estimates are determined mostly by the carrier phase observations (for long enough spans of continuously connected phase data).  Allan variance results can be used to assess the precision performance of the carrier phase clock estimates.

GPS CARRIER PHASE FREQUENCY TRANSFER ON THE NIMA MONITOR STATION NETWORK

 

B. W. Tolman, and D. Munton

Applied Research Laboratory, University of Texas

 

ABSTRACT

 

The National Imagery and Mapping Agency (NIMA) maintains a worldwide GPS Monitor Station Network (MSN). Each site is equipped with a high quality geodetic receiver and a Cs frequency standard, and a core set of sites have secondary (backup) receivers and frequency standards which share (only) the GPS antenna. The NIMA MSN produces a variety of data products, including 1.5 second and 30 second GPS range and phase observations from the core stations, and phase-smoothed pseudoranges on 15-minute epochs. The latter are used in producing precise satellite ephemerides. Applied Research Laboratories, the University of Texas at Austin (ARL:UT) provides the lifecycle development engineering for the NIMA MSN. Interest in monitoring the behavior of frequency standards for maintenance purposes, and in examining the higher data rates, has motivated us to apply the technique of carrier phase time transfer to the NIMA data.

ARL:UT has developed analysis software to study carrier phase time and frequency transfer. This software processes the raw 30-second dual frequency data in a continuous sequential estimator, which allows unlimited forward processing as well as optional backwards (fixed-interval) smoothing. Data is typically processed in 24-hour segments and smoothed. States in the estimation include tropospheric and ionospheric delays, corrections (assumed small) to the station positions and double differenced carrier phase biases. Satellite positions are taken from either precise or broadcast ephemerides.

The goals of our work are to study time and frequency transfer within the NIMA network of GPS tracking stations for the purpose of monitoring the clock behavior, investigating sources of error and noise and estimating their magnitudes, and to study precise time transfer techniques using GPS carrier phase data. We present an overview of our methods, preliminary results of the processing and a discussion of hardware effects that might be the dominant source of noise in the final results.

For the time being, we are searching for an optimum strategy for steering the maser in order to transfer the intrinsic stability and accuracy of CSF1 to TAF(PTB).

GPS-BASED ADAPTIVE ESTIMATING OF TIME ERROR FOR CLOCK SYNCHRONIZATION WITH FIR FILTER

 

Y. Shmaliy, A. V. Marienko*, R. Rojas-Laguna, and O. Ibarra-Manzano

Guanajuato University, Mexico, *”Sichron” Center, Kharkiv, Ukraine

 

ABSTRACT

 

Finite impulse response (FIR) filtering of time error, also named moving averaging (MA), is widely used as well as the Kalman filter in timekeeping to discipline the clocks. Of all the known filters, a simple MA with a constant weight 1/N, where N is the number of points in the average, is best in sense of produced noise. And, since the signal exhibits the stationary nature, a simple MA is most accurate in terms of errors. In the nonstationary case, the weight of the MA filter is optimized to eliminate the bias that is inevitably achieved at result of noise, so that any optimally unbiased FIR filter produces noise bigger than a simple MA.

For the sake of accuracy in timekeeping, one would like to use the estimator, which guarantees the smallest values of all the filtering errors, namely root-mean-square error (RMSE), bias, and maximal error. To obtain such an estimator, it seems now that it is best to solve an adaptive problem for the GPS-based time error measurement. The problem formulates in the following way:  Let an optimal stochastic FIR filter be designed to estimate a time error, then adapt the filter weight to the slope of the time error function aiming to obtain the smallest estimated errors.

In the report, we discuss the results of such an adaptation of the optimal stochastic MA filter. We present the adaptive algorithms for the time error optimal estimates and show that accuracy of the adapted optimal filter strongly depends on the slope estimate. In practice, since the slope of the clock time error function is caused by the dynamics of the fractional frequency shift (environment, aging), then adaptive optimal stochastic FIR algorithms are readily designed separately for crystal, rubidium, cesium, and hydrogen clocks.

We examine the adaptive optimal FIR filters for the test time error signals corrupted with Gaussian noise and for the GPS-based measurements (Motorola GPS UT+ Oncore Timing receiver) obtained with the disciplined crystal oscillators and rubidium standards. We show that the adaptive algorithms for the rubidium standards seem more accurate than those for the crystal oscillator. The reason is the more predictable behavior of the frequency of the rubidium standard. An error analysis is given and many plots of the original and filtered processes are discussed in detail.

Abstract

 

The optically pumped atomic-beam clock offers the potential for orders-of-magnitude improvement over conventional beam clocks. Poster 11

 

ROBUST GPS-BASED SYNCHRONIZATION

OF CDMA MOBIL NETWORK

 

Dominik Schneuwly

Oscilloquartz SA, Switzerland

 

ABSTRACT

 

                Mobile communication networks based on the CDMAOne and CDMA2000 standards require that the base-stations of their radio access networks be synchronized. All the base-stations of a network need a 1PPS phase reference with a 3 ms accuracy. The Global Positioning System is currently the only practical way of implementing this type of synchronization. Mobile communication networks also require a high degree of availability, since safety critical user applications rely more and more on public mobile networks. Although the GPS has an excellent system availability track record, there is always a real possibility of radio interference at some base-station sites. In order to protect a base-station against failures caused by radio interference problems, the base-station’s clock must be able to provide the 1PPS phase reference for some time under failure conditions affecting the satellite to receiver chain. The usual way of achieving this is by using the clock’s holdover mode. An alternative is to use a clock that can be slaved not only to the GPS as a primary reference, but also to an auxiliary external signal. This additional signal is extracted from the transport network. SONET transport networks work with synchronous (in terms of frequency) optical carrier signals. The idea is to derive a frequency reference signal from that optical carrier, and to use this reference to drive the base-station clock in case of failing GPS-reception. This paper proposes a two-phase protection concept for the base-station’s phase clock. In case of a GPS reception problem, the phase reference signal is maintained using the auxiliary frequency reference signal. When the latter fails (double failure), the clock eventually enters a holdover mode. The paper analyzes the performance of the two protection modes, and compares them to the requirements of CDMA-based mobile networks. The central questions are: “For how long can the required 1PPS accuracy be maintained under failure conditions?”, and: “ Under what environmental conditions must such a holdover performance be provided?” The analysis takes into account the stability of the frequency reference signal derived from the transport network, and the holdover performance of typical internal oscillators. The result shows that the two-mode protection significantly increases the time of effective protection. The paper goes on with the description of possible architectures for the synchronization distribution in the transport network. The emphasis is on an architecture based solely on GPS-receivers for the synchronization of the transport network and the mobile communication network. It is shown how this architecture enhances the availability of the auxiliary frequency reference derived form the transport network. These results shed some light on the way CDMA-based mobile communication networks can increase their availability. Such solutions are very important since the presence of the phase reference is vital for the operation of a base-station.
